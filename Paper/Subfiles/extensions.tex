\section{Extensions}\notinsubfile{\label{sec:exts}}

\par In this section, I describe the data and accompanying statistical model used to understand the role of \textit{financial literacy} in the current story of an empirical relationship between trust and returns.

\par Additionally, the literature on trust and social capital is clear on how heterogenity in income, race and other factors related to one's neighborhood can be important for trust. To this end, we consider two additional variables in the survey: respondent \textit{region} and \textit{hometown population size}. 

\subsection{Financial literacy and returns}

\par Financial literacy can be an important factor when trying to understand the persistent component of returns. The literature on measuring financial literacy using survey data has agreed on three questions (the ``Big Three'') to measure financial literacy for respondents. The first asks about compound interest: given \$100 in a savings account at 2\% per year, how much would you have after 5 years---less than \$102, exactly \$102, or more than \$102? The second asks about inflation: if the interest rate is 1\% and inflation is 2\%, after one year could you buy more, the same, or less than today? The third asks about risk diversification: whether the statement ``buying a single company's stock usually provides a safer return than a stock mutual fund'' is true or false.
