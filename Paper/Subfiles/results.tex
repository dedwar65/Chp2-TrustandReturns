\subsection{Determinants of trust}

\par Table~\ref{tab:trust_reg_general} shows the empirical models for trust examined in this paper. As \cite{Alesina2000} suggests, demographic variables tend to determine trust. In the HRS data, the estimated coefficients for the non-hispanic, black race variable and for marital status seem to have the strongest significance. The sign for the race coefficient is negative, which fits the exposition in \cite{Alsan2018}. Years of education also has some predictive power.

\par The full set of controls leans on the literature regarding other variables which may predict trust, such as health outcomes, insurace statuses, and pervious marital history. Each of these may have a link with trust in theory, but the coefficient for Depression (and less so for Medicaid coverage) is negative and particularly predictive of trust.

\subsection{Trust and economic performance}

\par After examining the determinants of trust, I focus the analysis of the empirical relationship between both income and returns and trust on a subset of measures. For income, I omit the analysis for labor income. I also exclude the analysis of the log of total income. Labor income results are not statistically significant. Given the oversampling of older, retired households, lack of predictive power for trust on labor income is not alarming. Total income results are robust to both log and IHS transformations.

\par For returns, I omit core and core and retirement portfolio results and instead focus the statistical analysis on returns to net wealth only. The results for the smaller portfolio compositions are largely insignificant across specifications (though the results are more encouraging when retirement assets are excluded). Again, with more retirees in the sample, an analysis of retirement assets may take more creative efforts. That said, I show significant that trust is predictive for the raw measure of returns to net wealth, and the relationship is robust to different trimming and winsorization choices.\footnote{This is important given the difficulty with computing returns in the U.S. due to lack of available data on flows in and out of narrowly defined asset classes. }

\subsection{Returns from 2002-2022}

\par The main empirical analysis of this paper involves computing returns across several years and describing the persistent component of returns. As my motivation is characterizing heterogeneity in the rate of return across individuals, I use model specifcations similar to those in \cite{aflgdmlp20}. First, a pooled OLS regression with a baseline set of controls. Second, a similar pooled regression aimed at controlling for risk exposure. Each of these specifications are captupred by the linear panel data regression model \[r^{net}_{i,t} = \alpha +  X_{i,t}'\beta + \varepsilon_{i,t}\] where $X_{i,t}$ the set of regressors includes both time-varying and time-invariant demographic variables, as well as the trust measure as linear or with both linear and quadratic terms present. 

\par The third specification has two stages. I begin with a panel regression including individual and year fixed effects along with the time-invariant regressors from the second model. This regression model is extended so that the error term $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ can be written as \[\varepsilon_{i,t} = f_{i,t} + e_{i,t},\] where $e_{i,t}$ may possibly be serially correlated. Then I regress the estimated individual fixed effect onto the time invariant regressors \[\hat{f}_{i,t} = \bar{X}_{i,t} + u_{i,t},\] where $\bar{X}_{i,t}$ is the set of time-invariant regressor (subset of the full set of controls). This will give us a sense of how the persistent component changes with the variables excluded from the first stage.

Moreover, the descriptive statistics for mean returns show that returns do not seem stable over the time period (ex.\ Global Financial Crisis). To account for risk exposure in this environment, I add full interaction terms between shares invested in given asset classes (core, retirement, residential) and year. I now turn attention to estimating the persistent component of returns by estimated the model with individual fixed effects. Notably, inclusion of this term will require we drop the time-invariant regressors from the set of control variables.

\par Employment status and marital status are significant predictors of returns to net wealth when fixed effects are included. The asset share variables (core, IRA, residential) and debt shares (long-term and other) are also jointly significant, confirming the importance of portfolio composition even after controlling for individual fixed effects.

\par In terms of the estimated fixed effect, Table~\ref{tab:panel_reg_r5_spec3_win} shows that a large share of total variance is captured by $\rho$, which is evidence in favor of heterogeneity in returns across respondents. \par By contrast, $\sigma_e$ captures the remaining within-person time variation in returns that is not explained by observed controls. The within-$R^2$ is therefore interpreted as the share of within-respondent return movements explained by the included time-varying covariates -- about only a third of the within-person variation in returns is left after controlling for observables, and importantly, individual fixed effects.

This pattern is consistent with a small group of households exhibiting persistently higher net-wealth returns, while most households cluster around near-zero persistent effects.

\subsection{The ``right amount of trust'' in the HRS}

\par The model specifications including both a linear and quadratic term for trust are of the form \[r^{net}_{i,t} = \alpha + \beta_1 \textit{Trust}_i + \beta_2 \textit{Trust}_i^2 + X_i'\gamma + \varepsilon_i\]. Taking the partial derivative with respect to trust is \[\frac{\partial r^{net}_{i,t} }{\partial \textit{Trust}_i} = \beta_1 + 2\beta_2 \textit{Trust}_i\]. Setting this to zero yields the critical point \(\textit{Trust}^* = \frac{-\beta_1}{2\beta_2} \). When \(\hat\beta_1 \geq 0,\hat\beta_2 \leq 0\) the relationship is hump-shaped (concave) and the estimated level of trust which maximizes returns to net wealth is \[\textit{Trust}^* = \frac{-\hat\beta_1}{2\hat\beta_2} \].

\subsubsection{The maximal level of trust for income and returns}

\par Table~\ref{tab:trust_turning_points_original_results} reports the turning point \(\textit{Trust}^*\) for each specification, along with \(\hat\beta_1\), \(\hat\beta_2\), whether both coefficients are individually significant, and the joint test p-value.

\inputtable{../../Code/Regressions/Other/trust_turning_points_original_results.tex}
\label{tab:trust_turning_points_original_results}
\FloatBarrier

\par Across all six specifications, \(\hat\beta_1 > 0\) and \(\hat\beta_2 < 0\), so the relationship is hump-shaped and \(\textit{Trust}^*\) is a maximum. Both trust coefficients are individually significant in five of the six specifications: total income in 2022, average total income, average returns to net worth, and returns to net worth in panel specifications 1 and 2. The Wald test for the linear and quadratic trust terms is rejected at the \(5\%\) level in average total income, average returns to net worth, and both panel specifications. Estimated trust \(\textit{Trust}^*\) associated with maximal economic performance ranges from about \(5.3\) for average total income to \(7.4\) for returns to net worth in the baseline panel specification, and in every case it lies within the observed trust scale \([0, 10]\).
